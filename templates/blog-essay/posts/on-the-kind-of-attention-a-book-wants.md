There is a kind of reading that is not compatible with a phone in the same room. It is a form of attention that thickens slowly over the first twenty minutes and then holds itself, without effort, through the next hour, and leaves you — if you are lucky, if the book is the right one — slightly changed.

This essay is about that attention, and about how quietly it disappears.

## I

I used to read for three hours in the evening. Two now would be a good night. I can see, by looking at the receipts of my own time, that the difference is not &ldquo;less time&rdquo; — the difference is that the time I have has broken into smaller pieces. A twenty-minute span followed by a glance at a phone is not a twenty-minute span. It is twenty-one minutes that arrived as several ten-second chunks.

The book does not know this. It asks of me the same kind of attention that it asked of my grandmother. She read for whole afternoons. I read in intervals. The book treats me the way a book would, and I keep noticing the loss of some chapter I already closed.

## II

I bought a small wooden box from a cabinet-maker in Évora for the price of a cheap book. It sits on the kitchen table. When I sit down to read, I put my phone in the box and close the lid. When I stand up to make tea, I leave it there.

This is not a productivity system. It is a box.

What the box has given me back, over six months, is an evening. The tenth page of a novel, on a Tuesday, with my phone in a box in another room, is not an abstract experience any more. It is a room I have returned to.

## III

The internet has, with a patience that looks from this angle almost loving, trained me to read in the form of the internet. To scroll. To extract. To skim for the kernel. I am quick at it. I am quick at a great many things I did not used to be quick at.

The novel does not reward skimming. The novel rewards the long arc of a sentence and the slow turn of a paragraph, and — in a way that I keep having to learn again, after every interruption — the reader who forgets what the reader is currently trying to extract.

A novel is a book you cannot be quick at.

## IV

What I am arguing, if I am arguing anything, is that the shape of the attention a book wants is not a shape the rest of our lives will hand to us for free. We have to notice that it is a shape. We have to spend, on some small ritual, the cost of choosing it.

The cost is not large. The payoff is that the books you read at twenty keep becoming the books you read at forty, and you find, a chapter at a time, that you never stopped being the kind of person who could sit with them.

That is most of what I have to say.
