The last ten percent of anything is the hardest. I have known this for years; I forget it in every new project. Here are some notes I am writing so that next time, at least, I will have read them.

## I

Beginning is not hard. A new project has the charm of a new room. It holds open possibilities; every choice is reversible. You make mess and you call it progress. For a while, you are correct to call it that.

## II

The middle is easier than it looks. By then, you have a shape to return to. The decisions of the first weeks have set a grain, and the work is to go with the grain. Most days feel productive. Most days are productive.

## III

The last ten percent is where the wood gets harder. This is not an accident. The last ten percent is where every small decision that felt reversible is suddenly permanent. It is where the essay commits to being this essay; the software to being this software; the notebook to being this notebook. The earlier freedoms are gone.

What that costs you, psychologically, is much larger than the work itself. The physical effort of the last ten percent is small. The imagination it requires is not.

## IV

The only thing that has ever worked for me, at this stage, is to show up at the desk at the time I said I would show up. Not to &ldquo;feel inspired.&rdquo; Not to &ldquo;find flow.&rdquo; To sit down at nine and leave at eleven, whether the two hours yielded a page or a single clean sentence, and to come back the next day regardless.

Finishing, I now think, is the habit of returning to a nearly-finished thing.

## V

The most honest thing I can say about finishing is that I still do not like it. I miss the open room of the first week. I miss the grain of the middle. I don&rsquo;t miss them enough to stop finishing — but I notice, every time, the small grief of closing a door you spent a year leaving open.

Write this down somewhere you will find it at the end of the next project. That is the only good reason to write any of it.
