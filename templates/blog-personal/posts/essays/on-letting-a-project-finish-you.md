When you finish a project, the project does not just stop. It stays in you for a while. It alters, in small and specific ways, the shape of the person you are at the desk on the first morning of the next one. I have been thinking about this, and about the cost of noticing it.

## A short list, by way of illustration

I finished a piece of software in 2021. By the time I was done with it I had acquired:

- A near-religious respect for migrations that run backwards.
- An insistence on explanatory commit messages.
- A quiet aversion to any library whose maintainer has not written a README in full sentences.

None of these were beliefs I held when I started. They came out of the work like a trail of sand falling out of a shoe — not in the big moments, but while walking.

## What the book did

I wrote a book in 2018, and the book made me a slower reader. A slower reader, specifically, of paragraphs I previously would have skimmed. I can now feel, with a kind of embarrassment, the pace at which my eye used to cross a page. It has never fully gone back.

## What the studio did

Three years of running a small studio have made me allergic to a meeting that could have been an email. They have made me allergic to an email that could have been a pull request. They have made me allergic to a pull request that could have been a written note on the pull request. Each allergy is downstream of a specific bad experience, and the allergies have accumulated in me like the small, fussy preferences of an old person.

## The point I am trying to land

The trade every long project is actually making with you is not the trade you think it is. The trade is not &ldquo;my time for this finished thing.&rdquo; The trade is &ldquo;my time, for this finished thing, and for the new person I will be when the thing is done.&rdquo;

It is worth it, I think. I also think it is worth knowing, before you start.
