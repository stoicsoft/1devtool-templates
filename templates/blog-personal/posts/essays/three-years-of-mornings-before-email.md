On a Monday morning in January 2023 I decided to stop checking email until after lunch. I made the decision lightly. I did not tell anyone about it. I did not even write it down in a notebook. I just did it, for one day, and then did it the next day, and then kept doing it for three years.

Here is what I noticed.

## Most emails do not need a morning

Ninety percent of the email I was checking at eight o&rsquo;clock in the morning did not need to be answered by eight o&rsquo;clock in the morning. Most of it did not need to be answered that day. A fraction needed to be read that day. The rest, it turned out, had been occupying my attention in exchange for nothing.

## A morning that begins without email is shorter

The morning felt shorter in the first week, longer in the third. I think what was happening is that the email had been compressing my mornings into a series of small, reactive windows. With the email gone, the morning regained its original shape. A single, long task fit into it.

## My colleagues adjusted in about a fortnight

I had expected pushback. I got, instead, a polite re-calibration. Within a fortnight, the rhythm of asynchronous responses from me had changed, and the people I work with had adjusted. Urgent things found me through Slack or text. Everything else, which was almost everything, waited.

## Three years in

I do not think I will go back. What I have built, accidentally, is a three-hour block at the beginning of every day where the only thing happening is the work I chose the night before. That block has paid for itself, and then some.

I am not going to tell you to do it. I am telling you I did, and that it worked, and that it cost nothing. The rest is your call.
