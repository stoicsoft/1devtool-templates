The first time a paper mill sends you samples, you think the differences are imagined. A 120 gsm uncoated cream sheet from one mill feels, to the hand, almost identical to the 120 gsm sheet from the next mill over. You write on both. You print a page on both. You close your eyes and the differences begin to emerge.

## What the fibres remember

Paper is a record of its river. The cellulose remembers the altitude it grew at, the minerals in the water it was pulped in, the temperature of the drying rollers. A cream stock from Viana looks, against a window, ever so slightly pinker than a cream stock from Gaia. The pink is not decorative. It is the iron in the water.

This is the reason a notebook you&rsquo;ve held for a year starts to feel like a thing from somewhere, rather than a thing from a warehouse.

## Our six-month search

We knew what we wanted. Uncoated. 120 gsm. Fountain-pen friendly. A warm cream, not an apologetic yellow. Smyth-sewn.

We ordered samples from fourteen mills across northern Portugal and France. We wrote the same four sentences on every sheet, in the same two pens, and set them side by side on the studio window for a month.

In the end, the choice was not the best-performing paper. It was the paper we kept wanting to pick up.

## A short glossary

- **GSM**: grams per square metre. Higher is heavier, not always better.
- **Uncoated**: no top layer; accepts ink directly, looks soft.
- **Laid**: subtle ribbed texture from a wire screen in the mill.
- **Wove**: smooth; the default for modern stock.

The Field notebook is printed on 120 gsm wove cream stock, milled in Santa Maria da Feira, bound in Porto. The next spring edition uses the same paper. We are not looking for a better one.
