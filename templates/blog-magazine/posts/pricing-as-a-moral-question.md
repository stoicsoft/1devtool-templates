The pricing page is the most honest page on your website. The landing page makes a claim; the pricing page settles it. If you want to know what a company really thinks of its customers, open their pricing page on a Tuesday morning and read it with the voice you use for a contract.

## The first year we charged

For most of our first year we gave the product away. Not as a loss leader. Not as a growth tactic. Out of a kind of instinct that charging would be rude — that nobody had asked for this, so nobody should pay for it. We thought we were being generous. We were being avoidant.

When we finally added a paid plan, we priced it so low that the first customer wrote back to ask if it was real.

## What a fair price is

A fair price is one that closes the circle between what you made and what the person needed. It is rarely the smallest possible number. It is almost never the largest. It is the number that, when you say it out loud in a meeting, you don&rsquo;t flinch.

- Cheaper than it should be makes everyone uncomfortable.
- More expensive than it should be makes the wrong people your customers.
- Right is quiet, and hard to find, and usually takes three rounds of change.

## We raised our prices in February

Forty percent across the board. We sent one email. We didn&rsquo;t offer the old price to anyone asking. We kept every customer we had.

What I learned is that customers, when you charge them honestly, develop a strange loyalty — not because they want to pay more, but because they sense that you will still be here next year.

> The goal of a price is not to convert. It is to match.

## A last note

We raised our prices again in September. It was, again, the right move. We didn&rsquo;t send an email that time.
