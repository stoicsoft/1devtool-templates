The flat was on the second floor of a building that had lost its numbers. The landlord, a woman of seventy who described Évora as &ldquo;a slow town, for people who don&rsquo;t want to be found,&rdquo; left the key under a blue pot by the door and wrote me a note in three languages saying the coffee maker needed patience.

I was there for a week.

## A morning routine, by accident

The first morning, I made coffee and opened my laptop. The coffee cooled before the laptop loaded. By the third morning, I was making the coffee, reading a page of Pessoa, and drinking the coffee before the laptop was open. By the fifth, the laptop didn&rsquo;t come out until ten.

It took exactly four mornings for a rhythm to install itself. I did not ask it to.

## On walking in July

Évora is not built for shade. The walls reflect heat back at you with a kind of institutional indifference. The correct time to walk is either seven, when the light is still soft, or half past nine in the evening, when the city gives back what it took all day.

I walked both ends of those days. In the middle I read.

## What I didn&rsquo;t do

- Instagram.
- Open email more than twice.
- Make a to-do list.
- Take a photograph of the flat for anyone.

What I did instead was notice the particular shade of pink the tiles turned at six forty-five in the evening, and try to write a sentence about it. The sentence is still wrong. The shade was correct.
