I went to Madrid in March to make portraits. The plan was to spend four afternoons in neighbourhoods I had not photographed before — Carabanchel, Vallecas, Tetuán, La Latina — and to ask, in slow Spanish, whether the people I met would let me make their picture.

What surprised me was how often the answer was yes.

## A small protocol

Years of street photography have given me a protocol for portraits. It is small. It is roughly:

- Stand near, but not at, the person you want to photograph. Look at the same things they are looking at.
- When you are near enough to be a person rather than an idea, ask. Use their language, even badly.
- Show them the back of the camera. Always.
- Send them the file. Not next week. Tonight, before you sleep.

The protocol is not a formula. It is a way of being present enough that the portrait does not feel like theft.

## What I noticed

People in the neighbourhoods I went to had less of the suspicion I had braced for. The bartender in plate No. 5 made me a coffee, then asked if I would also make one of his daughter, who was at school. I came back two days later and made the portrait of his daughter. It is plate No. 9.

## The light

April in Madrid gives you a kind of white-edged sunlight that is hard to work with at noon and beautiful between five and six. Most of the portraits in this feature were made in that one-hour window, in narrow streets that catch a bounced light off pale walls.

The whole feature was shot on the GFX 100S with the GF 63 — a slower, deliberate body that asks you to be sure before you press the shutter. Madrid was kind to it.
