Comporta in late March, before the season starts, is a quiet beach with a wide, slow tide. I drove down on a Friday and stayed at a small hotel run by a couple who told me the tide would be at its lowest at five-eighteen on Saturday morning.

I set the alarm for four.

## The setup

Long exposures of water want a few things to be true at once. The light has to be thin — pre-dawn is ideal. The wind has to be either still or constant, never gusting. The horizon has to be exactly level, because at thirty-second exposures even a degree of tilt looks like a mistake. A heavy tripod helps. A remote shutter helps more.

I used the Leica M11, a 14-stop ND filter, and a tripod that does not collapse easily.

## What the long exposure does

Long exposures of water do not photograph the ocean. They photograph the average of the ocean — the thirty-second mean of where the water has been. The result is a smoothed surface that looks more like a fog than a sea, and a sky that, if you have timed it right, holds the deepest blue of the morning.

The plates here are between thirty and ninety seconds. The longest, plate No. 4, was the last one I made before the light came up enough to wash everything out.

## The walk back

I stayed on the beach for another hour after the light got bright, drinking coffee from a thermos and not making any pictures. The hotel breakfast started at eight; I was back in time to eat it slowly, with a book open and the camera on the table beside me, where it could see the sea through the window.
